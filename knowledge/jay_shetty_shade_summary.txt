Source: jayShetty_ShadeZahraiPodcast.pdf (Jay Shetty x Shadé Zahrai, "Big Trust") — condensed notes for in-app coaching.

Core ideas (paraphrased):
1) People wait to "feel confident" before acting, but confidence typically comes AFTER action. Action gives the brain proof/evidence points.
2) The opposite of self-doubt is self-trust. Confidence (from Latin "con" + "fidere") ≈ "with trust".
3) The brain craves certainty and tries to conserve energy. It often magnifies what could go wrong so you avoid action. This is the "misguided protector".
4) Catastrophizing can feel safer than uncertainty because "at least you know" what will happen, but it keeps you safe-and-stuck.
5) Overthinking/anxiety can be linked to higher awareness of complexity and risk; the cycle can be interrupted.
6) "Spiral interrupt" idea: notice the threat response, name it, and deliberately shift attention to what you can control now.
7) Simple self-talk can help: "This is my brain doing what it does. I am safe to act anyway."
8) Re-engaging prefrontal attention reduces fear-center activation and restores rational choice about the next step.
9) Self-trust = trusting you can handle outcomes (success, failure, feedback) and recover.
10) Tiny, repeatable actions are the fastest way to rebuild evidence of capability and safety.

Lesson framing for users:
- We’re not trying to erase doubt; we’re training the ability to act with doubt present.
- Each tiny experiment is a "proof point" that makes self-trust more automatic over time.
